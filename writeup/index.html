PostcodeR

Real data on your real estate

#SummerDataChallenge entry by @benjaminlmoore, University of Edinburgh, UK




Data

The dataset I chose lists house sales within 100 km of London dating from January 2009 up until May 2014, a total of almost 1.4 million listings. The challenge is to generate actionable insights from these datasets, leading to novel products or services. To quote the competition spec, they're looking for:

new insights … and propose how these new insights can be used to create social or economic value in the form of new products, services, or businesses.

A good start is to visualise the data. Here's a time series of all the sales in the dataset, showing their 100 km radius around the City of London. The brightness of each dot is proportional to the price of the house sale.

We can zoom in on just the city itself, where most of the action is. It's possible to see hints of seasonal trends, and perhaps a gradual accumulation of brightness throughout the time series, indicating price rises â€” but we're getting ahead of ourselves.

Insights

So who's interested in this data? How can we create value and useful insights from this nice dataset. In short: homeowners. Prospective homeowners, landlords, real-estate investment trusts (REITs), private investors, mindful first-time buyers, local housing authorities…

Each of these groups can derive benefit from data-driven insights into their local housing market, including any observable trends, relation with their ward and district, future projections and neat summary statistics. At the time of writing, this data is not available to the consumer market in any usable form.

Thus, there exists business potential in providing a comprehensive and well-presented statistical analysis of a given area, such as a postcode. What follows is a case study for a single such identifier, explaining in detail the kind of insights we can glean from this house-sale dataset, as well as other publicly-available datasets, to provide a high-value service to end-users.

It has not escaped my notice that such a service could add significant value to existing house sales aggregators such as Zoopla, RightMove et al.. Indeed, such an add-on that requires data science expertise could be particularly valuable to such established web platforms.

Price rises

How have prices changed through this 5 year window? Importantly, where have the increased the most, and where the least? Can we extend trends to future predictions? Possibly there's some point in this short time period where prices appear to stagnate, when sellers might do better to wait a month or two in order to acheive the best price.
